How to Find and Hire a Reliable Contractor in the US

By RenovateIQ · 10 min read · Contractors

Finding a reliable contractor is one of the most important decisions you'll make on any home renovation project. The right contractor delivers quality work on time and on budget. The wrong one can turn your dream renovation into a nightmare — leaving you with substandard work, cost overruns and legal headaches.

This guide walks you through every step of finding, vetting and hiring a reliable contractor for your home renovation in the US.

Where to find contractors

The best contractors are usually found through personal recommendations. Ask friends, family and neighbors who have had recent renovation work done — a firsthand recommendation from someone whose home you can actually see is worth more than any online review.

If you don't have personal recommendations, these platforms are the most reliable for finding vetted contractors:

  • Houzz: Strong for kitchen and bathroom contractors, with verified reviews and project photos
  • Angi (formerly Angie's List): Large database of local contractors with reviews and background checks
  • HomeAdvisor: Good for getting multiple quotes quickly from pre-screened contractors
  • NARI (National Association of the Remodeling Industry): Find certified remodeling contractors in your area
  • NAHB (National Association of Home Builders): Directory of professional builders and remodelers
  • Better Business Bureau: Check contractor ratings and complaint history
💡 Pro tip

Ask your local lumber yard or tile supplier who their best customers are. Suppliers know which contractors consistently do high-quality work and pay their bills on time — both signs of a reliable professional.

Verifying licenses and insurance

This is non-negotiable. Before hiring any contractor, verify three things:

State contractor's license

Most states require contractors to hold a license to perform renovation work above a certain dollar amount. Licensing requirements vary significantly by state — California has strict statewide licensing, while some states leave it to individual counties. Check your state's contractor licensing board website to verify a contractor's license status and any disciplinary history.

General liability insurance

This covers damage to your property caused by the contractor's work. Ask for a certificate of insurance and verify it directly with the insurer — don't just accept a document the contractor hands you. Make sure coverage limits are adequate for your project size.

Workers' compensation insurance

This covers workers injured on your property. Without it, you could be personally liable for medical costs and lost wages if a worker is injured during your renovation. Some states exempt small contractors from this requirement, but it's always better to hire a contractor who carries it.

💡 Pro tip

Never hire a contractor who can't produce current certificates of insurance. A legitimate contractor will have these documents readily available and will have no hesitation in providing them.

Getting quotes

Always get a minimum of three quotes for any significant renovation project. Prices can vary by 30-50% between contractors for identical work — not always because one is better than another, but because of different overhead structures, current workloads and how much they want the job.

How to get accurate quotes

The quality of the quote you receive depends on the quality of the information you provide. Before asking for quotes, prepare:

  • Detailed plans or drawings of the work you want done
  • A written scope of work listing every task you expect completed
  • Material specifications where you have preferences
  • Your desired timeline
  • Whether you're supplying materials or want the contractor to supply them

What a good quote looks like

A professional quote should be itemized — breaking down labor and material costs separately for each element of the work. A single lump-sum quote makes it impossible to compare contractors meaningfully or understand where cost differences come from.

The quote should also clearly state what is and isn't included, the payment schedule, the project timeline and what happens if additional work is discovered (a change order process).

Red flags to watch for

Learning to spot the warning signs of an unreliable contractor can save you from a very costly mistake:

  • No physical address: Legitimate contractors have a business address, not just a cell phone number
  • Unusually low bid: A bid significantly below the others usually means something will be cut — either materials quality, labor hours or both
  • Pressure to decide immediately: A good contractor doesn't need to pressure you — they have a full schedule
  • Large upfront payment required: Asking for more than 10-15% upfront is a red flag, particularly for smaller projects
  • Cash only: Legitimate contractors accept checks or electronic payment and provide receipts
  • No written contract: Never proceed without a detailed written contract
  • Can't provide references: Any contractor who has been in business for more than a year should have satisfied customers willing to speak on their behalf

Checking references

Always check references — and actually call them, don't just collect the names. When speaking to past clients, ask:

  • Was the project completed on time and on budget?
  • How did the contractor handle unexpected problems?
  • Was the job site kept clean and organized?
  • Were subcontractors professional and respectful?
  • Would you hire them again?

If possible, ask to visit a completed project in person. Seeing the quality of work firsthand is far more valuable than any online review.

The contract

Never start work without a detailed written contract. A comprehensive renovation contract should include:

  • Full scope of work — everything that is included and excluded
  • Material specifications — brands, models, grades where relevant
  • Start and completion dates with milestone schedule
  • Payment schedule tied to milestones, not calendar dates
  • Change order process — how additional work will be priced and approved
  • Warranty terms on both labor and materials
  • Dispute resolution process
  • Contractor's license number and insurance details
💡 Pro tip

For larger projects over $50,000, consider having a real estate attorney review the contract before signing. The cost is minimal compared to the protection it provides.

Payment schedule

How you structure payments significantly affects your leverage throughout the project. A standard payment schedule for a major renovation:

  • 10% deposit at contract signing
  • 25% at project start — after demo and rough work begins
  • 25% at midpoint — after rough plumbing, electrical and framing inspection
  • 25% at substantial completion — when the project is largely done
  • 15% retention — held until the punch list is completed and you're satisfied

Never pay ahead of schedule. Your payment leverage is one of the most powerful tools you have to ensure work gets completed to your satisfaction.

Managing your contractor during the project

Hiring a good contractor is only the beginning. How you manage the relationship throughout the project matters just as much.

  • Visit the site daily if possible — staying visible shows you're engaged
  • Communicate in writing — follow up verbal discussions with a text or email summary
  • Document everything — photograph progress and any issues as they arise
  • Address problems immediately — don't let issues fester
  • Approve change orders in writing before any additional work begins
  • Keep a running punch list from day one

Finding a reliable contractor takes time and diligence, but it's time well spent. The difference between a good contractor and a bad one can be tens of thousands of dollars and months of your life. Do the work upfront — check licenses, verify insurance, call references and get everything in writing — and you dramatically increase your chances of a successful renovation.
