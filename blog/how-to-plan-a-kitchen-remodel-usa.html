How to Plan a Kitchen Remodel From Start to Finish

By RenovateIQ · 10 min read · Kitchen Remodel

A kitchen remodel is one of the most rewarding home improvement projects you can undertake — but it's also one of the most complex. With the average US kitchen remodel costing between $25,000 and $75,000, getting the planning right before a single tile is laid can save you thousands and weeks of unnecessary stress.

This guide walks you through every stage of planning a kitchen remodel, from setting your budget to the final punch list.

Step 1: Define your goals and scope

Before you spend a penny, get clear on what you actually want from your new kitchen. Ask yourself:

  • Are you doing a full structural remodel or a cosmetic refresh?
  • Are you moving any walls, windows or doors?
  • Do you want to change the layout or keep the existing footprint?
  • What's driving the remodel — functionality, aesthetics or adding value to your home?

A cosmetic update — new cabinet doors, countertops and appliances — can be done for $10,000–$20,000. A full structural remodel with layout changes can easily reach $75,000+. Knowing your scope upfront prevents scope creep, which is one of the biggest causes of overspending on remodeling projects.

💡 Pro tip

Walk around your current kitchen for a week and note every frustration. Too little storage? Poor lighting? No island? These pain points should drive your brief, not just how you want it to look.

Step 2: Set a realistic budget

Kitchen remodel costs vary enormously depending on the size of your kitchen, the quality of materials, your location and the complexity of the work. Here's a rough breakdown of where the money goes:

  • Cabinets and countertops: 40-50% of total budget
  • Labor: 25-35% of total budget
  • Appliances: 10-20% of total budget
  • Flooring and backsplash: 5-10% of total budget
  • Electrical and plumbing: 5-10% of total budget

Always add a 15-20% contingency on top of your total estimate. Unexpected costs are almost inevitable — hidden plumbing, structural issues, or simply changing your mind about materials mid-project.

💡 Pro tip

Get at least 3 quotes from kitchen contractors. Prices can vary by 30-50% for identical work. Always ask for itemized quotes so you can compare like for like.

Step 3: Check permits and local codes

Most kitchen remodels require permits, especially if you're moving walls, changing the electrical panel, adding circuits, or relocating plumbing. Permit requirements vary significantly by city and county — what requires a permit in Los Angeles may not in a rural area.

Never skip permits to save time or money. Unpermitted work can cause major problems when you sell your home, may void your homeowner's insurance and could require you to undo completed work. Your contractor should handle permits, but always confirm this upfront and ask to see permit documentation before work begins.

Step 4: Plan your layout

The kitchen work triangle — the relationship between your sink, range and refrigerator — is the foundation of good kitchen design. The three most popular layouts are:

  • Galley kitchen: Cabinets on two parallel walls. Great for narrow spaces and serious cooks.
  • L-shaped kitchen: Cabinets on two adjacent walls. The most versatile layout for most homes.
  • U-shaped kitchen: Cabinets on three walls. Maximum storage and counter space.

If your budget allows, hire a kitchen designer to draw up your plans. Many kitchen retailers offer this service free when you purchase through them. Alternatively, use a free tool like IKEA's kitchen planner or the National Kitchen and Bath Association's design resources to experiment with layouts before committing.

Step 5: Choose your materials and appliances

This is where most people overspend. It's easy to fall in love with the premium option at the showroom, but there are excellent mid-range alternatives for almost every component.

Countertops

Quartz offers the best combination of durability and low maintenance and is the most popular choice in US kitchens right now. Granite is still a classic option. Butcher block adds warmth but requires regular sealing. Laminate has improved dramatically and can look virtually identical to stone at a fraction of the cost.

Cabinets

Stock cabinets from home improvement stores are the most affordable option. Semi-custom cabinets offer more sizing flexibility. Full custom cabinets are the most expensive but allow complete design control. Shaker style remains the most popular and timeless choice.

Appliances

Appliances are one area where it pays to invest in quality — they get used every day. Prioritize a good range and refrigerator. You can save on the dishwasher and microwave.

Step 6: Find and vet your contractors

The quality of your contractors will make or break your remodel. For a kitchen, you'll typically need:

  • A general contractor or kitchen remodeling specialist
  • A licensed plumber
  • A licensed electrician
  • A tile setter (if not included with the GC)

Always verify that contractors are licensed in your state and carry both general liability insurance and workers' compensation. Check reviews on Houzz, Angi or the Better Business Bureau. Ask for references from recent similar projects and follow up on them.

💡 Pro tip

Never pay the full amount upfront. A standard payment schedule is 10% deposit, progress payments tied to milestones and 10% on completion after the punch list is signed off. Some states legally limit the size of upfront deposits — check your state's contractor laws.

Step 7: Create a project timeline

A typical full kitchen remodel takes 3-6 weeks depending on complexity and contractor availability. Here's a realistic timeline:

  • Week 1: Demo, rough plumbing and electrical, drywall repair
  • Week 2-3: Cabinet installation, appliance rough-in
  • Week 3-4: Countertop templating and installation, backsplash, flooring
  • Week 4-5: Appliance installation, finish plumbing and electrical, painting
  • Week 5-6: Final inspections, punch list, touch-ups

Build in buffer time for appliance delivery delays and material lead times. Custom cabinets can have 8-12 week lead times, so order early.

Step 8: Prepare for living without a kitchen

This is the part nobody plans for. Set up a temporary kitchen area with a microwave, coffee maker and mini fridge. Stock up on easy meals, paper plates and a realistic takeout budget. Warn your neighbors about dumpster deliveries and construction noise.

Step 9: Manage the project

Even if you're using a general contractor, stay involved. Visit the site daily if possible, keep a running punch list from day one and photograph everything before walls are closed up — you'll need those photos if problems emerge later.

Track every expense against your budget in real time. It's remarkably easy to let small additional costs accumulate unnoticed until you're suddenly 20% over budget.

Step 10: The punch list

Before making your final payment, walk through the finished kitchen methodically and note every imperfection — cabinet doors that don't align, grout that needs cleaning up, scratched surfaces. A good contractor will address punch list items as part of the contract. Get everything resolved before releasing the final payment.

A well-planned kitchen remodel adds significant value to your home and transforms daily life. The key is doing the groundwork — clear scope, realistic budget, vetted contractors and a solid project plan — before any work begins.
